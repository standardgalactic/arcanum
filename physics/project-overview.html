Lagrange’s Theorem and Cosets In ordinary (left/right) coset theory, for a subgroup H ≤ G, we have: ∣G∣ = ∣H∣⋅∣G/H∣ ⇒ |H| ∣ |G| This is Lagrange’s Theorem, which states that the order (or size) of a finite group G is always a multiple of the order of any of its subgroups H. This is due to the partitioning property of cosets: each element in G belongs to exactly one left (or right) coset of H.

The proof hinges on two key observations: 1. Each left (or right) coset gH (or Hg) has the same number of elements as H, i.e., ∣gH∣ = ∣H∣ and ∣Hg∣ = ∣H∣ for any g ∈ G. This is because the map h ↦ gh (or h ↦ hg) from H to gH (or Hg) is a bijection, preserving cardinality. 2. The collection of all left cosets {gH | g ∈ G} (or right cosets {Hg | g ∈ G}) forms a partition of the group G. In other words, every element in G belongs to exactly one left (or right) coset of H, and no two distinct cosets have any elements in common.

From these observations, we can deduce Lagrange’s Theorem: since each coset has |H| elements and the set of all cosets partitions G, it follows that ∣G∣ = n⋅∣H∣ for some positive integer n. This integer is exactly ∣G/H∣ (the index of H in G), which divides ∣G∣ by definition.

Intuitively, Lagrange’s Theorem says that the “building blocks” (subgroups) of a group determine its “size” up to a factor given by their indices—like how a number’s prime factors uniquely identify it, aside from the power they are raised to.
