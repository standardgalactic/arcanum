### Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) is a significant concept in calculus that establishes the relationship between differentiation (derivatives) and integration. It essentially bridges these two major operations by showing they are inverse processes, providing a powerful tool for solving problems involving both accumulation (integration) and instantaneous rates (differentiation).

1. **The First Part of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:**

   This part demonstrates that differentiation and integration are inverse operations. In simple terms, if you have a function f(x), you can create a new function A(x)—the area under the curve of f from some constant 'a' to x. The FTC states that the derivative of this area function (A'(x)) is exactly equal to the original function (f(x)) at any given point x.

   Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

   d/dx [∫_a^x f(t) dt] = f(x)

   In essence, if you look at how the area under a curve is changing as you move along the x-axis (through differentiation), you're essentially finding the height of the curve at that point.

2. **The Second Part of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:**

   This part provides a method for evaluating definite integrals using antiderivatives, which simplifies many calculations. It states that if we have a continuous function f(x) on [a, b], and F(x) is any antiderivative (indefinite integral) of f(x), then:

   ∫_a^b f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

   This means that to find the definite integral from a to b of function f(x), you simply evaluate an antiderivative of f at point b and subtract the value of this antiderivative at point a.

In summary, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is crucial because it establishes the connection between rates of change (derivatives) and accumulated quantities (integrals). It allows us to solve problems involving accumulation and rates in a more straightforward manner by transforming between these two perspectives. This theorem essentially underpins many applications of calculus, from physics to economics.

🔷 1. Lagrange's Theorem and Cosets
In ordinary (left/right) coset theory, for a subgroup H ≤ G, we have:
∣G∣ = ∣H∣⋅∣G/H∣ ⇒ |H| ∣ |G|
This is Lagrange's Theorem, which states that the order (or size) of a finite group G is always a multiple of the order of any of its subgroups H. This is due to the partitioning property of cosets: each element in G belongs to exactly one left (or right) coset of H.

The proof hinges on two key observations:
1. Each left (or right) coset gH (or Hg) has the same number of elements as H, i.e., ∣gH∣ = ∣H∣ and ∣Hg∣ = ∣H∣ for any g ∈ G. This is because the map h ↦ gh (or h ↦ hg) from H to gH (or Hg) is a bijection, preserving cardinality.
2. The collection of all left cosets {gH | g ∈ G} (or right cosets {Hg | g ∈ G}) forms a partition of the group G. In other words, every element in G belongs to exactly one left (or right) coset of H, and no two distinct cosets have any elements in common.

From these observations, we can deduce Lagrange's Theorem: since each coset has |H| elements and the set of all cosets partitions G, it follows that ∣G∣ = n⋅∣H∣ for some positive integer n. This integer is exactly ∣G/H∣ (the index of H in G), which divides ∣G∣ by definition.

Intuitively, Lagrange's Theorem says that the "building blocks" (subgroups) of a group determine its "size" up to a factor given by their indices—like how a number's prime factors uniquely identify it, aside from the power they are raised to. 

🔷 2. Failure of Analogous Results for Double Cosets
When transitioning to double cosets HxK = {hxk | h ∈ H, k ∈ K} ⊆ G (where H and K are subgroups of G), we encounter a significant departure from the Lagrange-like behavior observed in left/right cosets. Unlike single cosets, double cosets do not necessarily have the same size or partition G evenly.

The key reason for this failure is that the "coset action" by H and K does not generally behave as nicely as in the left/right coset case:
1. The size of a double coset |HxK| can vary with x ∈ G, unlike single cosets where |gH| = |H| for any g ∈ G. 
2. Double cosets do not always partition G uniformly; different double cosets may have differing numbers of elements, and their union might not equal G unless additional conditions (like normality) are met.

As your example with S3 illustrates, the size of a double coset need not divide the order of G, nor do all double cosets necessarily have the same size. 

🔷 3. Normality as a Savior: When H or K is Normal
While general double cosets exhibit erratic behavior, normality of either subgroup H or K provides structure and recovers some Lagrange-like properties:
1. If H is normal in G (H ⊴ G), then the set of all double cosets H\G/K forms a partition of G, similar to left cosets. In this case, |HxK| = |HK| for any x ∈ G, and if G is finite, |G| = |HK|⋅|H:K|, where |H:K| denotes the index of K in H (i.e., the number of cosets in the set H/K).
2. Analogously, if K is normal (K ⊴ G), then the set of double cosets G/K\H forms a partition of G, and |HxK| = |HK|. 

Normality ensures that the stabilizer (conjugation action) of one subgroup on the other respects the group structure, enabling a uniform decomposition of G into coset-like structures. This recovery of orderly behavior highlights how normality imposes significant algebraic constraints, counteracting the wild fluctuations observed in arbitrary double cosets.

In summary, while left/right cosets adhere to clean multiplicative rules governing their sizes and partitioning of the group, double cosets can exhibit much more erratic behavior. However, normality of one or both subgroups restores order, aligning double coset properties more closely with those of standard cosets—albeit in a potentially more complex manner involving indices instead of simple equality.


A double coset is a concept in group theory that generalizes the notion of cosets (left or right) to involve two subgroups instead of one. If H and K are subgroups of a larger group G, then the double coset of H and K with respect to an element x in G is defined as HxK = {hxk | h ∈ H, k ∈ K}.

Unlike single cosets (left or right), which divide the group uniformly into equal-sized subsets, double cosets do not necessarily partition the group evenly. This means that the size of a double coset does not always divide the order (number of elements) of the larger group G. Furthermore, different double cosets can have varying sizes.

This phenomenon occurs because double cosets take into account interactions between two subgroups H and K. These interactions can lead to uneven distribution across the whole group, whereas single cosets are simply partitions based on a single subgroup's action.

To illustrate this concept with an example, consider the symmetric group S₃, which consists of six elements: e (identity), (12), (13), (23), (123), and (132). Let H = ⟨(12)⟩ (the subgroup generated by (12)) and K = ⟨(13)⟩. 

- The double coset HeK is computed as {hxk | h ∈ H, k ∈ K} which results in {e, (12), (13), (132)}. This double coset has 4 elements.

- Similarly, H(23)K = {hxk | h ∈ H, k ∈ K}, resulting in {(23), (123)}. This double coset has only 2 elements.

In this case, neither of the double cosets' sizes (4 and 2) divides the order of S₃ (which is 6). This exemplifies how double cosets do not partition a group evenly in terms of cardinality, unlike single cosets guaranteed by Lagrange's Theorem.

   - H\G/K ≅ G/HK
   - H\G/K ≅ HK\G

   |G| = |HK| * |H\G/K|

   This implies that both |HK| and |H\G/K| divide |G|.

3. **Reasons for Double Cosets' Misbehavior**: When H or K are not normal, the usual divisibility logic fails due to:
   - The action of H × K on G via (h, k)⋅g = hgk⁻¹ doesn't ensure uniform orbit sizes.
   - Sets HxK may overlap in complex ways unless H or K is normal.
   - Stabilizers and orbits vary in size unlike single-sided coset partitions.

   |HxK| = |H| * |K| / |H ∩ xKx⁻¹|

   This formula shows that even in finite groups, the size of a double coset depends intricately on how H and the conjugate of K intersect.

The Theory of Orbits and Quotients is a crucial concept in modern mathematics, interweaving several disciplines including group theory, geometry, representation theory, and algebraic topology. This theory fundamentally explores how group actions partition sets and the creation of "quotient spaces" that encapsulate these symmetries.

1. **Group Action**: Let's denote a group as G and a set as X. A group action is defined as a function from the Cartesian product of the group and the set (G × X) to the set itself (X), written as (g, x) ↦ g⋅x, satisfying two key conditions:

   - **Identity**: The identity element 'e' in G leaves every point in X unchanged. That is, e⋅x = x for all x ∈ X.
   - **Compatibility**: For any group elements g and h in G, the action of h on g's result equals the result of the combined action. Mathematically, this is written as g⋅(h⋅x) = (gh)⋅x.

2. **Orbits and Stabilizers**: 

   - **Orbit**: For a given point x in X, its orbit under G, denoted O_x or G⋅x, is the set of all points that can be reached by applying elements from G to x: {g⋅x | g ∈ G}. In simpler terms, it's the collection of points x can be moved to via actions of G.
   
   - **Stabilizer**: The stabilizer of a point x in G, denoted Stab_G(x), is the subgroup of elements that leave x unchanged: {g ∈ G | g⋅x = x}. This forms a subgroup of G, often denoted G_x.

3. **Orbit-Stabilizer Theorem**: In the finite case, this theorem states that the size (or cardinality) of an orbit is equal to the index of its stabilizer in the group: |O_x| = |G| / |Stab_G(x)|. This theorem provides a powerful tool for understanding the structure of group actions.

4. **Quotient Spaces**: A quotient space, denoted X/G or X mod G, is constructed by partitioning the set X according to the orbits of its elements under the action of G. Specifically, it's the collection of all orbits: {Ox | x ∈ X}. The quotient space thus represents a sort of "collapsed" version of X where points in the same orbit are identified as one point in the quotient.

In essence, the theory of orbits and quotients allows mathematicians to study complex structures (like groups acting on spaces) by decomposing them into simpler parts (orbits), understand the symmetries involved (stabilizers), and ultimately form new mathematical objects (quotient spaces) that encapsulate these symmetries. This framework is instrumental in various fields, including representation theory where it aids in understanding how groups can be represented by linear transformations on vector spaces, and algebraic geometry for studying quotients of algebraic varieties.


In the category-theoretic language, the action of group G on space X can be encapsulated using an **action groupoid**, denoted as [X/G]. This is essentially a categorical construction that allows us to consider not just the set of orbits but also the stabilizer information.

Objects (Obj): Each object in this category corresponds to an element in the space X, i.e., for each x ∈ X, we have an object (x) in [X/G].

Morphisms (Mor): The morphisms in this groupoid are generated by the group action of G on X. More specifically:

  - For any g : x → y in G and corresponding morphism from object x to y, we have (g,x) : (x) → (gx).
  - For every x ∈ X, there's an identity morphism id_x : (x) → (x), which corresponds to the action of the identity element e ∈ G on x.
  - Given g1, g2 : x → y in G and their corresponding morphisms (g1,x) : (x) → (gx) and (g2,x) : (x) → (gx), their composition is defined as ((g2 ∘ g1), x) : (x) → (g2 * g1 * x).

This groupoid [X/G] captures the orbit information of G's action on X. The objects are essentially the orbits themselves, while the morphisms encode how elements in G move points between these orbits. 

The category-theoretic perspective enables a more nuanced treatment of quotients, especially when stabilizers are nontrivial. A Category Fibered in Groupoids (CFG) is a concept in category theory, closely related to the notion of a fiber bundle in topology. It's a functor that satisfies certain conditions, allowing for the study of objects and morphisms over another category in a way similar to how a fiber bundle organizes objects above base points.

**Definition:**

Let C be a category, and G be a category (often called the "base category"). A Category Fibered in Groupoids over C is a functor P: C^op → G^op, where C^op denotes the opposite category of C, satisfying the following conditions:

1. **Transport:** For any morphism f: x -> y in C, there exists a functor P(f): P(y) -> P(x), called the transport or pullback functor, such that for every object z in P(y), P(f)(z) is an object in P(x).

2. **Identity:** For every object x in C, P(id_x) (the identity morphism of x in C) is the identity functor on P(x).

3. **Composition:** Given a composable pair of morphisms f: x -> y and g: y -> z in C, the composite g ∘ f corresponds under P to the composition of functors P(g) ∘ P(f): P(z) -> P(y) -> P(x).

**Explanation:**

- **Objects and Morphisms:** Each object x in C is associated with an object P(x) in G, called the fiber over x. A morphism f: x -> y in C induces a functor P(f): P(y) -> P(x). 

- **Transport/Pullback:** The transport or pullback functor P(f) allows you to "pull back" objects and morphisms from P(y) to P(x), capturing the idea of how information changes when moving along a morphism in C.

- **Identity and Composition:** These conditions ensure that the association between C and G respects identity morphisms (P(id_x) is the identity functor on P(x)) and composition of morphisms (P(g ∘ f) = P(g) ∘ P(f)).

**Example:**

One common example of a CFG is the category of sets over a fixed set S. Here, C is the category of sets, and G is also the category of sets. The functor P: Set^op -> Set^op assigns to each set X the category P(X) whose objects are pairs (s, x), where s ∈ S and x ∈ X, and morphisms (s, x) -> (t, y) are defined only if s = t.

**Significance:**

CFGs provide a general framework for understanding how objects in one category vary over another, akin to the way fiber bundles describe varying structures over a base space in topology. They play a crucial role in descent theory and geometric objects like stacks and sheaves.
