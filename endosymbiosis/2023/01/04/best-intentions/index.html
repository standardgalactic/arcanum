Best Intentions

For the most part, people have good intentions. We generally want to be kind, responsible, and respected by our communities (at least, once our own personal needs are met). We have worthy goals and dreams for how to make life better. Yet, we all know that doesn’t always work out. We struggle with our impulses and willpower. We break our New Year’s resolutions as soon as we set them. We say one thing, then find ourselves doing another. This is true of individuals, but also for teams, communities, and organizations. Why does this happen, and what can we do about it?

Intentions are an important part of what makes us human. All sorts of animals set goals and make plans, but they’re usually very basic, short-term intentions, like “I’m gonna eat that bug.” In most species, lifestyle is largely innate, defined by genetics and the environment with only limited flexibility. Humans are different in that we design our lifestyles, both as individuals and as a society. We set abstract, long-term intentions like “I’m gonna save up for college” or “all people in this company should have equal opportunity.” These ambitious visions can help us imagine possible futures, and guide us to make them a reality.

It’s easy to set good intentions and stop there. Sometimes that’s enough, but often it’s not. Those delayed goals can be tricky for our animal minds, after all. I meant to pick up eggs on my way home, but I forgot. I know I ought to make a healthy dinner, but I’m exhausted, so I’ll order pizza. I said I’d learn Japanese, but maybe I was just kidding myself. I wanted to build a shed in the backyard, but I didn’t really know how, so it never got started. When one of my female engineers at work was criticized unfairly, I thought I was being supportive when I swooped in to defend her. When she pointed out how I was preventing her from proving herself, I had to unlearn that habit. In each of these cases, I meant well, yet I fell short.

This highlights some of the key problems with intentions. They aren’t rules that the brain enforces. If I keep my intentions in mind, I might notice when they should affect my decisions, and act accordingly. Or I might not. Like all people, I spend a lot of time on auto-pilot. I often get tired or distracted. I make countless choices in a day I’m not even aware of. Then there’s willpower. Just because I know I ought to do the right thing, doesn’t mean I will after a long and frustrating day. Lastly, intentions don’t come with step-by-step instructions. It’s often not clear how to achieve my goals, so I have to think about the steps involved, what my options are, and what outcomes are likely. Even if I do everything right, reality doesn’t always play along. I have to watch to make sure things turn out the way I intended, and respond when they don’t.

At their best, intentions serve as a frame for thinking and planning. They’re a first step in a process that leads to an outcome. To improve the chances of success, I must design that process. I must compensate for my cognitive blind spots and mitigate the risk of accidents and surprises. This is a form of self-programming. What can I do now to shape my future behavior? How do I make sure I get into the right situations and avoid the wrong ones? How will I notice when that happens? Can I prepare so that I know what to do in the moment, and have everything I need to act? How will I know if my plan is working, and change course if I need to?

I’ve found there are two essential tools for this sort of planning:

  • Write and revisit. Intentions are often too vague to be useful. They can be easy to forget, and can drift over time without our notice. To remedy this, I write my intentions down. When possible, I share them with others to make sure I’m rigorous about it, and to create a sense of accountability. Then I set a reminder to revisit those intentions in a few weeks or months. I try to be brutally honest with myself. Am I living up to my expectations? Do I still think about the problem in the same way? If not, I try not to feel guilty, but instead focus on what to do about it. What’s wrong? Should I change my behavior? My intentions? Both? I force myself to think about this.
  • Prepare yourself. When I set intentions, I think through how to achieve them. Not in full detail, but at least I’ll identify a few major sub-goals that are essential for success. I think about when and how I might do those things, and create some structure around that to make it real. I write TODO lists and set time aside for specific tasks. I leave notes or physical reminders in the real world to nudge me at crucial moments. I think about what decisions I’ll have to make, and how to make them. This is a way of front-loading the effort, doing the thinking and willpower work when I have time for it. That way, when the crucial moment comes, my actions can be fully automatic.

Critically, these two things go hand in hand. Writing down my intentions doesn’t help much if I don’t take some action to ensure they happen. Similarly, making a plan and following it rigorously can be a disaster if it’s the wrong goal, the wrong plan, or if the situation changes. The benefit comes from cycling through these two modes of thinking regularly. What should I do? How should I do it? Is it working? What should I do next?

The same problems appear in teams, communities, and organizations, often amplified dramatically. Aligning intentions across many people is very hard, and aligning behaviors is impossible. Each person has their own motivations, and is gifted and fallible in their own unique ways. To be fully effective, they must set their own intentions, and find their own ways to reliably achieve them. Many leaders are good at setting intentions for their groups, but struggle to make them happen. Sometimes they just say what they want to accomplish and assume everyone gets it and will (somehow) make it happen without any guidance or coordination. Other times they micromanage, trying to force people to deliver by robbing them of their autonomy.

I lead others the same way I lead myself. I just apply the concepts at different levels of abstraction. As a leader, my main responsibility is to keep intentions fresh in my team’s minds and to encourage each individual to be productive in their own way. Rather than telling people what to do and how to do it, I make sure my team has policies and tools to help with common challenges. This gives them freedom and flexibility, but also reduces their burden in the moment and encourages consistency. I create timelines, schedule check-ins, and set reminders in the right time and place. I often don’t care about the timing, I just want to give folks a nudge when they need it, and encourage them to prioritize, pace themselves, and track their own progress. Every quarter, we revisit our intentions together and try to think: are these still the right goals? Is our approach working? Is there a better way?

What makes humans so remarkable is our flexibility. We work creatively within constraints, figuring out the details as we go along. Intentions are a powerful tool for doing that, but they’re just part of the story. Acting intentionally, either as a person or as a group, is about creating the conditions for success. It’s not enough to want something. We’re just animals, after all. We can’t see the future, we can’t be “on” all the time, and it’s very easy to get distracted or to deceive ourselves. If we embrace our limitations, we can try to work around them and do better. Or, at least we can forgive ourselves more easily when we inevitably let that New Year’s resolution slip.

What do you think? Have you sometimes struggled to live up to your own intentions? Do you have any advice for how to overcome that? What about in the work setting? Have you noticed any practices that make a team / company better or worse at living up to their own ideals? I’d love to hear from you in the comments.

    This might sound a little arbitrary, but your post makes me think about my robotic vacuum cleaner.

    There are fancy robotic vacuum cleaners that have cameras, record where they’ve been, and plan the ideal route to methodically vacuum a room until it is finished. Then, there are the less expensive “dumb” robotic vacuum cleaners that bump randomly into every obstacle, causing them to aimlessly stumble through a room until a time limit has been reached.

    My robotic vacuum cleaner is one of the dumb ones.

    When I initially made the decision to buy one, I was set on getting a fancy one. Surely it would clean better than a dumb one! How could it not? It plans ahead and knows where it has been. It is optimal and vacuums with intent. The dumb vacuum… well… doesn’t.

    Before I made my final decision, I watched several comparison videos and read articles about the pros and cons of each, only to find something surprising. According to all of the comparison tests, the two vacuums performed the same. They both ended up vacuuming the entire room – every last inch of floorspace. Naturally, this caused me to choose the dumb vacuum, since it was cheaper.

    In this scenario, given the same set of constraints, the unintelligent and unintentional system performed equally as well as the intelligent and intentional system. I’m sure that the dumb vacuum takes longer, but as the constraints are tightened (the room is made smaller), the dumb vacuum’s time to completion approaches that of the smart vacuum. As an extreme example, you might imagine a space with no room to move, in which they would both finish at the same time.

    My comfort level with the dumb vacuum’s “uncontrollable” approach to cleaning might increase as the space grows smaller. Does it make sense to make think of this more generally? If something is out of my control, then it has its own freedom to do something. Thinking of teams – guiding people in a direction under their own volition… maybe I could say that the tighter the constraint, the more freedom they have to move within it.

      That’s a really interesting example. Thanks for sharing!

      You remind me of Daniel Dennett’s idea of “competence without comprehension,” which is a useful tool for thinking about intelligence. It’s totally possible to do something smart without any awareness or intentionality. The “dumb” robot vacuum is a great example of this. It just follows some dead simple rules, bumbling around randomly, because that’s all it needs to do to clean the room effectively.

      So why do we have intentionality at all? I suspect it’s about open-endedness, which you hint at in your comment. In small rooms without too much complex shape, both vacuums do equally well. Maybe the smart vacuum is more efficient, but it very are few opportunities for its cleverness to matter. If you had lots of obstacles, or hard-to-reach areas, or constraints on when and where the vacuum should run, well… then you’d need the smart vacuum. To reliably achieve those more complex goals, it would need to do some amount of planning, which would also mean a degree of self-awareness.

      Human beings tend to have big intentions in a chaotic environment, so being intentional and making plans is more important. If we just go around “reacting” like the dumb vacuum, we’ll make similar kinds of mistakes—we’ll miss some opportunities, do redundant work, get tangled up in obstacles, etc. Of course, that’s not a perfect analogy, because a person reacting in the moment can be fully aware of their situation and respond very cleverly. That’s one of the reasons why we can get away with just good intentions much of the time. Our ability to quickly judge a situation and improvise in real time is something no robot even comes close to yet.

    I have another thought about this post that might be more on topic. There are a couple of tricks that always seem to work for me when intending to start something new and sticking to it. However, they likely aren’t for everyone. Through conversations with friends and associates, I have found that most believe these strategies would produce the opposite effect for them. I wonder what you think of them.

    1. I am not starting something new. I have always done this. This is the way things are and the way they have always been.

    I started a simple at-home exercise routine when the pandemic started. I was doing a few familiar things late at night. I was accustomed to staying up late. I’ve been staying up until 2am or later since adolescence. It was a part of who I was, how I thought, how I operated. It was a part of my creative process and my unshakeable habit that helped make me who I was up to that point.

    As the months went on, I didn’t change my workout routine much and I wasn’t particularly consistent. I didn’t really care that much about it. It was just a way for me to feel better in the isolation of the early days of the pandemic. I recognized that it greatly helped my personal wellbeing, which is why I kept it up.

    At some point, I stopped. Things were getting busy at work and I had other personal projects I was working on – other things to keep me occupied in the late evenings. I stopped working out for months, but I kind of wanted to get back into it.

    So, almost to my own surprise, I decided to start waking up early. In the course of one week, I started waking up earlier in the morning each day until I reached my goal of waking up at 5:30am. On Monday morning when I awoke at 8am, I told myself that I had always done that and today was no different. On Tuesday, when I awoke at 7:30am, I told myself the same. As the days went on, I continued to tell myself that I this morning is no different than all of the other mornings of my life. I have always awoken early.

    I actually believed myself. I guess I can be pretty convincing – especially to myself.

    One morning, without having thought of it or planned for it prior, I decided to go into the gym. When I got in there, I told myself that this was my normal routine. I always went to the gym early in the morning, and today was no different.

    For my loved ones, this was shocking change in my routine. They said things like “you only just started waking up early” or “you just started to go to the gym”. No, I would reply, this is the way things are.

    I didn’t consciously plan ahead, but I knew that I had to change my behavior. Instead of working through the trials and tribulations of making a change, I skipped past it. I didn’t have to change, because I had always been this way… at least that’s what I told myself.

    2. I like this. I don’t like that.

    I find it easy to tell myself that I like or don’t like things. It’s weird how powerful that is.

    When I was younger, I loved ice cream. I would buy ice cream weekly, if not more. I had a habit of eating ice cream after dinner, or sometimes as a late-night snack. Ice cream is not great for my health and I knew that I should stop. So I just told myself that I don’t like it… and it worked.

    To this day, I rarely have ice cream… maybe once a year at most. I just don’t like it (so I say, and so I believe).

    3. Keep it a secret, or reveal few details. Don’t talk about it much.

    I always seem to have at least one personal programming (or other) project going at a time, but I find that I can never finish them. I’ve tried planning, no planning, and everything in between. For some reason, I always lose interest or run out of steam.

    More recently, I had a project that I actually completed… well… that is to say I got a functional MVP working to my liking. Once that was done, it too lost steam. However, before that happened, things were going great. I had actually been consistently working on it for months, which was by far better than any project of similar scope up to that point.

    Why had I gotten so far into this project and fallen from the others so much sooner? What was different about this situation? All things being similar to previous projects, there was one major difference: I didn’t tell anyone about it. I kept it a secret. I never talked about it.

    Interestingly enough, once I had completed the MVP, I started to tell people about it… and once again, the project slid into limbo with the rest.

    I feel like, at least for me, when I tell others about my intent, the telling grants me a sense of accomplishment. It frames it so well in my mind that I feel like I’ve already completed it, as if the implementation details are so easy to solve that they’re not worth doing… so I don’t do them… and so I don’t complete the project.

    I’ve found that if I want to complete something of significant scope, it’s best for me to wait until it’s complete before sharing. If I share too soon, I give myself a sense of accomplishment and completion prematurely, which prevents me from doing the real work to actually complete it.

The trick is not to “decide.” I just do it, every time, because that’s the routine. Maybe I’ll miss going to the gym some day, but that’s not because I decided to give myself a break. No, something messed with my routine, and tomorrow things will go back to normal! If I can put myself in that mindset, it’s much easier to make changes.

      I can also relate to #2. Instead of ice cream, my example is alcohol. I picked up the bad habit of binge drinking in college. As I grew older, the negative effects began to outweigh the positive ones, but it was really hard to break the habit. I found what worked for me was to change my mental associations. Whenever the idea of drinking comes up, I try to remember the visceral feeling of discomfort I get from drinking too much. I imagine that acid queasiness in my stomach, the clouded mind, the headache, the struggling to sleep. I try to really feel it in my body, and that immediately makes the alcohol less appealing. By doing this every time, it becomes automatic and I don’t have to think about it any more. It’s a powerful deterrent!

      #3 is different for me, but I find your example really interesting. The motivation for sticking with a hard task comes from dopamine, which is also associated with drug addiction. The dopamine system rewards a job well done with a feeling of satisfaction, but it also anticipates that feeling of satisfaction and gives you little dribbles of dopamine to keep you moving towards that finish line. It’s the physical manifestation of motivation. When you lose interest, the idea doesn’t produce any dopamine any more. Your brain stops rewarding you for doing the hard thing, so you don’t bother.

      Dopamine is a really tricky thing to work with, though. Dopamine levels can change suddenly and dramatically in response to all sorts of things, which differ from person to person. Karl, perhaps a big motivation for you with these side projects is to show people you’re creative and working on cool ideas. As long as that goal is still ahead of you, your body will supply the dopamine to keep you plodding forward. But if you “cheat” and give yourself release early by sharing the half-baked idea before it’s done, then suddenly there’s not enough anticipation to keep you going.

      It’s really powerful to understand these unconscious, internal dynamics that drive you. Learning to use them is like tricking your mind and body into doing what you want. Acting intentionally is hard, so I’m glad for every trick I can get!

I simply can’t break a pledge I’ve snapped on, and if I find I’ve violated such a pledge without thinking about it, I stop immediately and give myself an extra punishment in addition, such as an extension of the pledge. There are certain kinds of promises this works for, and certain kinds I would never snap on. I wouldn’t snap on “I will do X before I sleep” because that could lead to fighting sleep dangerously.
