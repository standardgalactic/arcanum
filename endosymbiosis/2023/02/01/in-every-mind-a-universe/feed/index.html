I admit my use of the word “ontology” is a bit non-standard. I guess I mean any symbolic model of reality, including domain-specific ones, personal ones, communal ones, etc. This was useful for the main point I wanted to make: we think of the world as really existing in a clear and well-defined way, but the way we parse the world is based on these models, there are lots and lots of them, and none of them is objectively true.

I think you bring up some good examples, like when people use words or acronyms that another person doesn’t know, or has a different definition for. That is what I’m trying to talk about, I’m just adding an extra layer of abstraction. Often we think of reality as being made up of objects, concepts, and categories. From that perspective, an ontology is like a master dictionary. It names all the things that exist, and every person has memorized parts of that dictionary, perhaps incorrectly. Instead, I’m arguing that reality exists, but objects, concepts, and categories do not. They’re just useful fictions we made up in order to organize reality, break it down, and make it less overwhelming. There is no master dictionary. Each person makes up their own, and the true miracle of human communication is that there’s enough agreement between them that we can talk about reality and (for the most part) understand each other unambiguously.

You bring up a cool point about how people might have fundamentally different ways of conceptualizing abstract ideas. We may agree on the word, or even be able to write compatible definitions, but the “raw form” of the idea in our heads might be radically different, perhaps using different modalities (visual vs. emotional) or brain regions. I think it’s pretty amazing to realize how much variation there is across minds, and how different our experience of reality must be. ðŸ™‚

Yes, the idea of ontologies as tools we pull out when we need them, but which may contradict each other, resonates with me. It reminds me of how we can see people on a very personal level, as they connect and compare to ourselves, but we can also see other people as part of general social groups and trends, or as animals with a few extra intellectual features, or as biological systems controlled by hormones and brains and evolution, or even as physical systems controlled on the atomic level by physical laws. The impressions we get from these levels of modeling may be hard to reconcile, or even contradict, but they can all be useful.

Likewise, I’m reminded of how there are adages and cliches that run contrary to each other in attitude, but one or another may be useful for different situations; one for when you need to be brave (“fortune favors the bold”), another for when you need to be cautious (“don’t count your chickens before they’ve hatched.”) To use one or another of these sayings is to select an ontology (I guess) for the current situation and use folk wisdom to fortify it.

I watched an old video of his that briefly sums up some of his key ideas: https://youtu.be/uArSolZX19U

That video, at least, is about whether the Universe is fundamentally physical or mental in nature. I’ll admit, I’m pretty biased towards the Materialist perspective, that reality is out there and our mental experience is a representation of that reality. That’s just how I was raised, and it’s the starting point for most science. However, I also see the appeal of Idealism. After all, our mental experience is all we have access to, and physical reality can only be inferred from that!

My first reaction to this is, why not both? I see the Universe as complex and multifaceted, and there are many ways to describe its reality that are equally valid. We can talk about reality as being built up out of matter, yet that matter is emergent from the interactions of fundamental forces on the quantum level. We can talk about reality in terms of meaning, intention, and relationships, yet none of those can manifest or interact without matter as a substrate. Which is more fundamental? Seems like a silly question to me. I think the important thing is that, even if you think of mind as emerging from matter, that doesn’t make it any less real. It’s “just” an ephemeral pattern in the properties of matter, not a physical thing itself, but so is a computer program or a mathematical concept. We experience those things, they influence reality, and they have practical value to us. I don’t see the value in arguing they are somehow less significant or valid because they aren’t concrete matter. Scientists focus on what is measurable, of course, but it would be foolish to say what we cannot measure does not exist!

Anyway, that’s my 2 cents, and some context for other readers who might want to check out his work.

Good point. You remind me of Representation and Reality, which argues very clearly that all language is self-referential in nature. It’s just a web of abstractions and relationships between them that may or may not be aligned with “reality,” which we do not have access to.

I think it’s an interesting and very plausible idea that most / all of the mind’s contents are ontological in nature. In that case, bare facts might just be degenerate categories that contain one member, with properties defined in terms of relationships to other facts. That reminds me of computational ideas like the lambda calculus, where whole computer programs, including numbers, can be expressed using functions.

If we take that perspective, then I think my previous comment is more about coarse vs. fine grain changes in ontology. In some cases, learning takes place exclusively at the fine-grain level, filling in the details of a well established representation of reality. In other cases, learning forces you to massively remodel your ontology, making room for lots of new facts and resolving all the most troublesome contradictions until you’re pretty good at intuitively understanding and predicting the behavior of the system.

So, I guess the impact of learning on your ontology depends on what you learn, and in particular on how diverse your education is. The structure you end up with reflects the learning process that constructed it and the dataset it was “trained” on.
