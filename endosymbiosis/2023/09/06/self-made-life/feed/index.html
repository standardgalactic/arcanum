]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2023/09/06/self-made-life/comment-page-1/#comment-208 Sat, 09 Sep 2023 12:46:47 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=199#comment-208 In reply to Thorin N. Tatge.

Very interesting thoughts as always, Thorin. Thanks for sharing!

This comment reminds me a lot of one of my favorite books on this topic, Incomplete Nature. For one thing, I think that’s where I get the idea of describing evolved traits as “purposeful,” though Dan Dennett talks about that a lot, too, in the form of “free-floating rationales.” In either case, the idea is that life has good reasons to do what it does, it just doesn’t necessarily know what those reasons are! They are just “what worked” in the context of natural selection. But they still serve a purpose (keeping the organism alive) and life chooses to do them (in the sense that an organism’s biology evolved to be one way and not another). We’re just used to doing these things in our minds, so we aren’t used to seeing them in a non-cognitive context, and feel uncomfortable describing both with the same word. Personally, I like using the same word, just to force us to think about what these different processes have in common, which is a lot.

The other interesting point you touch on is looking at life from multiple levels of description. Human minds like to think about “individuals” but that word can apply to cells, organisms, communities of a single species, whole ecosystems, or even all of life itself. Each of these things is a complex system with many parts bound together by their mutual relationship to each other, with collective behaviors that are very much more than the sum of their parts. I think the biggest difference between these is that reproductive fate is generally bound to an organism, not its cells, nor its community. That makes it difficult to talk about “selection” at those other levels of description. But I think that’s okay. The way we talk about selection should be different at the population level, because that’s a different kind of adaptive system. What we’re really talking about isn’t whether this population is better than that population, but rather what selective forces within the population shape its collective behavior, and how does that feed back to individual reproductive success. I admit, this explanation is a little half baked, but it’s something I’d like to develop more in the future.

One way of describing evolution that I love is “design by preserved accident.” So, your last question gets at something pretty fundamental, I think! The simple answer is that successful organisms have more kids, but this whole post is about why I think it’s more complicated than that. Arimaa is a great point of comparison. That game was designed to be a variation of chess that’s harder for a computer to solve than regular chess. One of the big ideas that I’m arguing for here is that life acts a bit like a game designer. Every single lifetime is like an instance of the game, where an organism does its best to “win,” figuring out what to do in real time, but always within the constraints of the “rules” embedded in its biology. But over long stretches of time, life also experiments with the rules themselves. Arimaa is a game designed to be hard for a certain kind of adaptive system to play, forcing programmers to find a different kind of adaptive system to beat human players. Life tries to design a game (many games, actually; at least one for each species) that is easy for a certain kind of adaptive system (a genetic cell) to play, so that the same core functionality can be shared across many different lifestyles. So, it’s doing the opposite of what the Arimaa designer did.

Tatge https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2023/09/06/self-made-life/comment-page-1/#comment-206 Sat, 09 Sep 2023 07:36:13 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=199#comment-206 So, cells as programmers! What do I think? Well, I think this analogy is fair. I guess the main meat of it is in how “life” gives its own cells guidance on how to change and improve their programs. And that’ll be covered in Part 3! So, what other smaller thoughts do I have for this part?

It’s interesting to think of life as having the purpose of figuring out how the universe can be thrived in. Strictly, I think purpose is a thing that implies a choice being made, in that for a thing to have a purpose, someone needed to make the thing with that purpose in mind. (The purpose of a hammer is to hammer nails.) For an idea to have a purpose, someone needed to conceive of the idea with that purpose in mind. (The purpose of voting is to give the people a say in their own leadership.) So, when someone talks about the “purpose of life” or the purpose of some particular evolutionary adaptation, they’re speaking loosely. I guess the assumption is that when we talk about the purpose of an adaptation, we’re saying “If this organism (or its designer) was specifically trying to help this organism be more fit, what was the purpose of including this adaptation?” So, our go-to “purpose” for living things is indeed (usually) that first one–living and reproducing. But occasionally, people talk about the purpose of something on a broader scale, like an ecology. They might say, the purpose of plankton is so the little fish and crustaceans have something to eat. Or even, the purpose of plankton is to transfer energy from the sun into animals, which can’t absorb its energy (in a usable way) directly. So, sometimes we do imply a higher-level purpose when it comes to living things.

We can see the whole multifarious story of life as a big project to figure out what works, biologically speaking, and what doesn’t. Oh, most of the universe is a vacuum but there are some atmospheres around some planets, and some oceans? That’s where life will work, check it out! Let’s try living things that have their liquid innards open to the world. Oops, it all spilled out! Okay, let’s try a version with a sealed membrane. Much better! We’re getting somewhere. Now let’s try a system where there’s a strict chain of energy transfer with no way to get the fuel back to the top. Oops, it just bottomed out and the creatures all died! Now let’s try a version where the energy loops around. Much better! And so on.

Viewed in this way, it does make sense to suggest that the evolutionary process of cells might be contributing to that broader, higher purpose as well as to the still exciting but “lower” purpose of keeping organisms alive. Why would things move in that direction, though? It seems like it would have to be what I mentioned in response to the last post–some kind of communal evolution, where it’s not about this organism versus that organism surviving, but this colony versus that colony, or this species versus that species, or this ecology versus that ecology. At the start of life, maybe even this biosphere versus that biosphere. High-level stuff! Where it’s harder to tell a compelling story about how the evolution happens and maybe harder to make the math work.

So, cells program by wading right into failure. How do they manage to keep their occasional successes, though? We were talking about Arimaa the other day and how it’s a game designed to make brute force searches really inefficient. Is the genetic code more like chess or checkers or Arimaa in that sense? If cells are being smarter than what brute trial and error would yield, how do those methods for being smarter work, and how did they develop?
