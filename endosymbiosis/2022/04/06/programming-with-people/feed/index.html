]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/04/06/programming-with-people/comment-page-1/#comment-35 Wed, 13 Apr 2022 15:09:38 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=62#comment-35 In reply to Thorin N. Tatge.

Interesting point about how this is a bit like what a collaborative artist does! Software engineering is definitely a creative activity, so we use a management strategy that is designed to bring out that creativity in people. I’m not sure how different it is in less creative jobs.

I love your examples of Real-Time Strategy (RTS) games and colonial insects. I think that’s really apt! Most people think of programming in the procedural sense (that is, a program is a sequence of instructions). What you and I are talking about here is constraint-based programming. In that model, the programmer describes the high-level properties and requirements for the system and lets the compiler (or person, or worker bee) provide the low-level implementation.

I think you point out an important point when you say many things work like this, but few can be told what to do. In some sense, all of life (and even some non-living systems!) are like constraint-based programs. The physical world provides constraints, and these systems find ways to work with those constraints to self-perpetuate. Humans and computers are special in that they get some of their constraints from explicit programming, rather than just the environment. That difference is what makes them capable of rapid and dramatic change.

I also love your game metaphor. In a sense, human culture is all about games. In a company, for instance, each job role is like a game. You are taught a certain way of looking at the world. This is your game board, or your heads-up display. There are rules to follow, valid and invalid moves, players to interact with, points to score, etc. What’s most interesting is that companies tend to make nested hierarchies of games. As you gain experience and get promoted to roles of greater responsibility, you learn to play new, more complex games. You coach people on your team to play the games you’ve already mastered, while to you these people become like “units” in your RTS. And, of course, your whole team is a “unit” to the next manager up, etc.

]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/04/06/programming-with-people/comment-page-1/#comment-31 Thu, 07 Apr 2022 14:26:22 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=62#comment-31 In reply to Dan.

You bring up an important point, Dan. There are many ways to define success, and I would argue some of them are deeply problematic. What I mean is, on average, happy well-supported people do better work. But there are other ways to extract “better work” from people that are not in the people’s best interest.

A classic example from the software industry is measuring an engineer’s performance in terms of volume of code written or number of bugs fixed. Those can be useful metrics for a team’s productivity, but if you make that the goal, it creates perverse incentives (see Goodhart’s Law). Either consciously or not, people begin to game those metrics, writing long-winded code and filing superfluous bugs just to close them. Those who focus on quality over quantity are punished for it. The numbers go up, but the quality of work goes down, and the employees hate it.

I feel like your examples are similar. Yes, surveiling employees and preventing them from organizing (like Amazon currently does, for instance) can improve certain performance metrics. It may even be better for a company’s stock price, in the short term. But my feeling is that if you look at the big picture, this is almost certainly worse. It hurts people’s lives, of course. But from the company’s perspective, it hurts employee retention, institutional knowledge, HR and recruitment costs, reputation, and more. Notice that playing the numbers game tends to improve things that are easy to measure, by shifting the losses into things that are much harder to measure.

I guess the behavior I’m advocating is to focus on people rather than numbers. If you support and empower your employees, and link their thriving to the company’s thriving, the numbers will go up. If instead you focus on pumping up your numbers, it may look better on paper, but it’s certainly worse for humanity and probably worse for your company, too. Of course, a big problem here is that our current economy strongly incentivizes companies to pump the numbers, so it’s no surprise that they do.
