You bring up a good point about walking being easier than it seems. Every body is shaped just so to make the common movement patterns as effortless and reliable as possible. This gives the cerebellum a lot of “hints” and makes it hard to screw up too badly! Originally this post was going to cover both the cerebellum and how the body itself learns to move, but that was way too much for one post. I may revisit the idea later, and this would be a good tidbit to include!

How does the cerebellum learn? Probably a lot like the rest of the brain, actually. There are some ways in which all neural networks are quite similar. Neurons that fire together wire together, which build networks of associations, which get adjusted based on feedback. The big difference is that the cortex has all these extra self-referential loops of analysis, which allow for more thoughtful behavior but also slow things down a lot. The cerebellum monitors itself and its signals, but it doesn’t contemplate what that means, what the consequences might be, whether there’s a better way, or how the future will unfold more than a few moments out. It’s got a much tighter feedback loop, is incapable of “overthinking” things, and can act very quickly without conscious thought. Like, when you casually brutalize a wave of microbes in Dr. Mario with nightmarish speed and precision, while your mind is focused on a pleasant conversation with a friend.

Is paying attention strictly necessary to learn? Maybe not 100%, but it certainly is if you want to get really good at something.

I’m particularly interested in how things that make us smarter in real time also make us better at adapting over evolutionary time. I think that’s really cool and important, so I’ll always bring up examples like that when I see them.

You didn’t miss it, it just came out! 🙂

In ones sense, I’ve been thinking about this for a long time. I’ve always been interested in the brain, and the cerebellum is a notable part. I was always curious about how some animals start off life being able to move and some don’t, or about people learning how to walk again after brain damage. It’s fascinating stuff.

Practicing yoga and getting physical therapy for an injury helped me see another perspective, though. I was never very athletic, so when I started learning to use my body I was shocked by what I didn’t know. For instance, I never really thought about the idea that you can perform the same gross movement using completely different sets of muscles, and you can learn to control which muscles you use. That’s obvious in retrospect, but really blew my mind. I was just “walking,” what do you mean I’m overusing my quads to do that? Similarly, I didn’t like to exercise because it “made my body ache.” Gradually I learned there’s a whole language of sensations that muscles use to describe their state (stiff, warmed up, tired, flexed, over-flexed, stretched, over-stretched, injured, repairing / growing, etc.) and you can learn to differentiate which individual muscles are sending those signal. Again, mind blown. Just by paying close attention, I turned this vague, ambiguous signal into something so much more precise and meaningful. Nobody could teach me that skill, because every body is different, and there’s no way to describe those sensations. That’s when I really began to think of the cerebellum as “a mind of its own” that you had to attend to and interrogate if you want to understand it.
