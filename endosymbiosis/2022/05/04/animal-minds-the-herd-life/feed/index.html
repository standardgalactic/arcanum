]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/05/04/animal-minds-the-herd-life/comment-page-1/#comment-44 Mon, 09 May 2022 17:24:31 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=79#comment-44 In reply to Thorin N. Tatge.

You’re full of interesting ideas, as always! Thanks for sharing. There’s lots I want to reply to. üôÇ

Let’s start with the horses. Life in a horse herd isn’t all drama and fighting. For the most part, it’s quite peaceful and fun. Teasing and harassment are common, but like in a school cafeteria, they’re usually pretty mild and folks mostly get along despite it. Even the nerds get to eat and run and play with everyone else. It’s just that everyone is socially aware, has a reputation to maintain, and must show deference where it is due.

On our farm, most of the horses lived in one big field adjoining the barn, the house, a road, and some woods. You’d think there wasn’t much to do, but that’s a great example of humans not seeing what matters to other animals. There was a lot of variety in how the horses would spend their time. Grazing was actually several different kinds of activity, depending on what foodstuff they were after. For horses, literally anything is potentially food. They will taste every grass, fruit, tree, fence, and water trough trying to find the best stuff. To the untrained eye, their pasture looked like undifferentiated green, but to them there was an enormous difference between clover, crab grass, and plantain. They learned which plants were delicious, which were gross, and which were more trouble than they were worth. They knew where different patches grew, when the herd ate there last, and who gets first claim when the plants grow back. Discovering the first patch of clover of the year would be a real boon for a horse, and an ethical dilemma: do you sneak a choice meal out of turn and hope you don’t get caught? Or do you alert the whole herd, even if that means you only get the leftovers?

Beyond grazing, there was watching the humans at the barn, watching the cars go by, socializing with the cats on the fence line, running for joy, playing chase, exploring the woods, rearranging the water troughs… the horses had plenty of things to do. What surprised me most was that the behavior of the herd overall would change dramatically depending on power dynamics. If one of the popular horses (call them Pat) liked to patrol the fence, they might lead the whole herd on daily circuits around the property. But if another horse (let’s say April) discovered a patch of fermenting apples, the herd might ditch patrol duty for a delicious boozy snack. Pat would lose social status and feel pressured to win it back. He might try to bully April and take over as king of the apples, but that might backfire if the herd saw him as a needy jerk. He might join the apple trend, deferring to April and biding his time for the season to change so he might reassert himself. He also might fall out of favor and never get the herd interested in patrolling again.

Did Dakota get frustrated and impatient being separated from the others? Perhaps. I was very young at the time, so I don’t remember those specific details clearly. In general, though, in this situation most horses would be nervous and apprehensive. Being alone is uncomfortable for a horse, and the main herd would be constantly watching her, calling out to her, judging her, and showing off. The herd’s display is meant to be intimidating, and the newcomer would be pressured to always be “on” in order to impress the others. That’s why we gave them a week to cool down and relax a bit before putting them together. As you say, it’d be really interesting to know how she was really feeling (was the bravado real, or just an act?), but I guess we’ll never know.

How similar are mammals, really? I admit, I over-simplified a little in my blog post for the sake of clarity. Emotions are part of an animal’s body, and every species has a very different range of bodies and emotions. My real point is that we’re much more similar than different. Many other species across the animal kingdom seem to be strongly emotional and in similar ways to us. I focused on mammals because I thought they’d be relatively uncontroversial. The farther you get from us in the family tree, the more different species seem, and the harder it is to see parallels. I didn’t want to argue about where to draw a line between species that “have feelings like us” and those that don’t, mostly because I don’t believe such a line exists, or that humans should judge other species by how similar they are to us.

I think comparing animals to young children like you do makes a lot of sense. Before we’ve absorbed our culture, people are much more like other animals. Just, keep in mind that children learn culture very quickly and without being explicitly taught. Once we learn the human stories about how to think, how things work, and why things are the way they are, our understanding of the world becomes much deeper and more complex than what animals have access to. We see past what is physically there to an imagined world of meaning that took our species collectively tens of thousands of years to uncover, describe, and accumulate. I believe animals are capable of similar feats of learning and comprehension as people, it’s just that they’re limited to what they can learn on their own, by observation, in a single lifetime. They aren’t dumb. They just can’t share their hard-earned knowledge and wisdom with each other nearly as effectively as people do, and so they don’t get the enormous head start that we’re privileged to.

I love your experience with Gerbils! That’s really cool, and it brings up another interesting point: human orientation. Some animals are very interested in human activities, and will engage with us in ways that seem very intelligent. Dogs are the prime example of this, and that should be no surprise: we made them that way through thousands of years of training and selective breeding. Some other species just don’t care about humans or the human world that much, which may make them seem dumb, antisocial, or incurious. Things would look very different if we could see them in their natural habitat, though. Every species engages very deeply with their lifestyle, their home environment, and (often, but not always) their kin, with strong instincts tied to particular objects and experiences they’re likely to find there. The problem is, people rarely get to see that, since we spend our time in very unnatural environments, and our very presence is usually a huge disturbance to other species!

But what about the bitty mammals with bitty brains? What about shrews and mousies? You get offended, you say, when people say “they can‚Äôt possibly comprehend the world like us.” Hmm. I often compare the reactions of smarter mammals, like dogs, to humans doing cultural/technological things, to the reactions of children watching adults do those same things. These adult creatures are people like me despite their godlike knowledge, and we are on good terms with each other, and I just have to wait for them to be done being adult humans. So. Smarter mammals, it seems, regard us like our own children do.

But what about Mongolian gerbils? My family used to keep and breed these when I was a child and teenager. My mother kept documentation of our family trees and I helped her work out the color genetics, along with various mailing lists and newsgroups on the relatively young internet. So while I never lived on a farm, I had extensive exposure to dozens of adults of this one species of mammal, plus hundreds of juveniles that we sold to pet stores. We definitely noticed that some of them were more intelligent than others. And they had personalities–bright, placid, grumpy, daring, friendly, skittish. But… did they comprehend the world like us? I think there were some big pieces missing from their intellect. If they watched us, I believe it was just in anticipation of us possibly picking them up or giving them food. They didn’t show curiosity in humans doing human things. I don’t think they knew one of us from another, or at least they didn’t seem to care. Some of them loved getting picked up to go on adventures (wouldn’t you if you lived in a cage?) and I was even able to train a few to climb up my arm with the excitement of it. But I don’t think they loved us as beings apart from the opportunity we represented. They loved each other as companions, mates, parents and children, but I think their empathy and comprehension didn’t go much beyond that. And gerbils are considered among the most intelligent rodents of their size, putting hamsters to shame.

I agree that it’s our advanced ability to convey and understand abstract symbols that really sets humans apart from animals. It’s also intriguing that computers are so good at that in some sense, and so very bad at it in another sense. Gary Marcus has some interesting things to say about that. He’s an AI researcher who argues (correctly, I believe) that deep learning represents only a small part of what the brain does. To make more meaningful advances, he says we’ll have to look at other aspects of our cognitive intelligence and behavior for inspiration.

He’s particularly focused on symbolic reasoning and problem solving, which makes a lot of sense to me. Despite appearances, even big language models that do quite well reading, writing, and answering questions about text don’t really “understand” words like people do. They are very good at recognizing and reproducing patterns in how words are used, but they lack even basic constraints about logic, causality, and common sense. Deep learning systems work on stereotypes rather than reasoning, and it’s startling yet limited success serves to show that human thought is often (but not always) like that, too. You may be right that learning how to do both in one system is where the magic lies! I find that prospect very exciting.

]]> By: Michael Demko https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/05/04/animal-minds-the-herd-life/comment-page-1/#comment-40 Sat, 07 May 2022 15:59:54 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=79#comment-40 Your story reminds me of a Scientific American article on chickens that I read a number of years back. Researchers knew that chickens had a variety of calls for specific situations, but it took some very rigorous observation to nail down evidence of how adaptable they were in making use of these calls to achieve subtle results.

One particularly interesting observation was that both males and females would make “warning, predator” calls based on how safely located they themselves were, and avoided making the calls if doing so might put them at unnecessary risk. But they would be more likely to make the calls if there were chicks around that needed to hear the warning, or if there were competing males in exposed positions who might be vulnerable to attack if the predator were to notice the group.

It is of course behind a pay wall, at: https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/the-startling-intelligence-of-the-common-chicken/, but at least one easily locatable copy has been made where the public might find it.

The general trend of animal intelligence research (AI?) seems to follow that of artificial intelligence (also AI?), the more we learn, the harder it is to find things that are easily identifiable as “special” to humans.

Relative to animals, the ease with which we can adopt novel symbolic references does seem to stand out. I could make up a word, tell you what it means, and you could immediately use it mostly-correctly, for instance. We do this all the time in corporate work settings. But arguably this is an easy thing for computers as well (depending on how you feel about the word “meaning”).

Relative to computers, the ease with which we integrate knowledge and experience from vast, often unrelated domains and synthesize non-terrible plans of action in novel situations seems to set us apart. But this seems to be a pretty standard capability for a lot of animals.

We are able to bridge both of these “forms of intelligence”, and somehow usefully tie them together. That seems to me to be where the magic lies.
