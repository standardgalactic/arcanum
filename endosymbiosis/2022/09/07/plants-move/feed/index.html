You’re right that a single organism can have many offspring, which amounts to a stronger positive signal for the population as a whole. I was thinking about something a little different: the “software” that receives the signal is a single individual organism. If you were born, then your parent(s) must have been doing something right, and that’s the only information you get until you evolve the ability to learn from your environment / parents after birth (higher intelligence). Both of these perspectives are correct and important. An organism can’t directly observe and react to population-wide effects (that requires communication of a sort), but a large population does amplify the noisy fitness signal by sampling it many times.

I should probably do a whole blog post about how life influences the trajectory of its own evolution, ‘cuz that’s a very big and subtle idea. Here’s a simple example, though: the Universe is the ultimate judge of an organism’s fitness, and evolution works by testing lots of ideas and having most of them shot down (literally killed) by the Universe. However, mate selection gives an organism the chance to use its full faculties to estimate how fit a potential mate is and how fit their children might be. If those estimates are accurate, then the organism gets to bias the next generation towards greater fitness. There will be fewer “wasted trials” where an obviously less fit individual predictably fails to reproduce. The search process overall will be more efficient, because it will anticipate and avoid dead ends, rather than wasting time and energy actually testing them in the Universe. The more intelligent life gets, the better its estimates are, and the more efficient the search becomes.

I agree, the main advantage of being sessile is saving energy. If it also makes evolution more efficient, great, but that should have a way more subtle impact on an individual’s fitness which would be very hard to demonstrate. Yet, I think this idea that “constraints are valuable” helps explain why sessile organisms can be so well adapted and responsive to their environments despite being so simple. When the environment is predictable, evolution can fine tune behavior very well, so that’s all you need. In a less predictable environment, you need to make more complex, open-ended judgments in real time, which requires more complex and expensive forms of intelligence (like brains). Part of why being sessile saves energy is because it allows you to get away with cheaping out and using evolution as your primary means of adapting to change.

I don’t actually know of anyone else talking about evolution in this way or making these sorts of predictions! That’s part of why I want to get into the field. Of course, I hope I’m not alone, and I’m actively looking for others who are researching these questions. I just don’t know how to find them yet. ðŸ˜›

]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/09/07/plants-move/comment-page-1/#comment-78 Mon, 12 Sep 2022 17:12:02 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=113#comment-78 In reply to Thorin N. Tatge.

Yes, I believe the process of evolution is itself intelligent. Though, this is where I’d like to have a better definition for the word “intelligent.” Basically, evolution is a kind of design process. It generates many possible ideas, tests them, and iteratively improves on the best ones. This seems intelligent to me, and it certainly produces intelligence. It’s relatively dumb, though, because it gets the minimal amount of feedback possible: Either “good enough” (reproduction) or no signal (dead). There’s very little anyone can do with that. The exciting idea that’s at the core of my research is that life recruits the full intelligence of each organism (through processes like mutation management and sexual selection) to get more of a signal to learn from, and make the iterative design process more efficient and intelligent. So, it’s not just that evolution is intelligent, but also that organisms can make observations and decisions that influence evolution and therefor accumulate over generations.

I’m no expert in plant communication, but it is fascinating. I’ll try to learn a bit more about it before I write a blog post. Certainly, mycelial networks and their interactions with trees would be a good topic to explore within that theme!

Most moving organism can’t choose not to move. Once you’ve committed to having a high-powered metabolism, you need to stay very active just to fulfill its needs. On the other hand, some species are bordeline. They haven’t over-committed to either strategy, and can thus switch on demand. Usually, they adopt a sedentary lifestyle when the environment is safe and abundant, and a mobile lifestyle when it’s not. Some species permanently switch strategies, but usually that only happens with the ones who are already in that borderline territory .

]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/09/07/plants-move/comment-page-1/#comment-73 Wed, 07 Sep 2022 18:09:41 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=113#comment-73 In reply to Diego.

Evolution is a powerful way to design complex systems that serve some purpose. It’s painfully slow, but it accelerates over time as innovations are preserved and layers of sophistication accumulate. It’s amazing how many intelligent behaviors are shared by all multicellular life. Humans (and all mammals, really) stand out because we have some of the most sophisticated real-time intelligence in the world. We have minds that model and refine the intelligent behaviors of our bodies as best they can, given their very limited awareness. They imagine past and future and produce a conscious perception of time. What they do is incredibly cool and powerful, but also wholly dependent on the foundation of our bodies, which set the limits of our abilities and handle all the “real work” of surviving in the world.

I think it’s awesome that Deep Learning research allows us to recreate some of the power of a mind. Yet, I see that technology as simulating a small patch of a single, disembodied brain. What it models and how it interacts with the world are defined by a programmer, and limited by that programmer’s creativity. All it can do is extract meaning and reproduce patterns created by some other intelligent system. For instance, the mind-blowing image generation algorithms in the news these days only work because they draw on images created and curated by people. In other words, the power of Deep Learning is dependent on human minds, which are dependent on human bodies, which are a product of evolution.

Personally, I’m profoundly moved by the idea that my conscious intelligence is just one small part of an enormously complex system, and a natural design program spanning billions of years. I think it’s really important to understand our minds in context.
