]]> By: Michael Demko https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2022/10/06/why-genetic-algorithms/comment-page-1/#comment-82 Thu, 06 Oct 2022 16:45:24 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=118#comment-82 You describe GA as “survival of the fittest”, but in a lot of ways neural network training is _also_ a process that can be described as “survival of the fittest”. The difference is in how the different agents (genome vs. neuron) vary relative to one another, and how they are rewarded.

In a neural network, there is a lot of positional variation (how the neuron is connected to others) but very little behavioral variation (what the rules are by which the neuron acts). “Fitness” is also positional, in that rewards that are available locally to one neuron are generally not available to another neuron elsewhere in the network, given identical behavior. The “reward” for fitness is increasing”influence” in the network – license to produce outputs with greater amplitude. “Survival” is expressed as “retaining influence” and “Death” is expressed as “influence that has dwindled to nothing.” In biological networks (neural or otherwise), linkages and cells that lose influence ultimately loose resources and literally die.

In a genetic algorithm, the variation is largely in the behavior of the agent, and agents may not have any meaningful position at all relative to one another. “Fitness” is more of a broad filter that applies evenly across all agents in the system. The reward for fitness is is not survival, but reproduction, another form of “growing influence” in the system.

Natural ecosystems involve _both_ forms of “survival of the fittest”: both the situational/accumulative (relativistic?) form and the behavioral/reproductive (universalist?) form, working in combination.
