There are lots of big organisms that don’t have brains, too. Think about trees, which can be enormous in themselves, but can also form super-organisms with other individual trees and a vast network of underground mycelium.

Why do I like the word “simulation” here? Well, think about the data available to your brain just for vision. Each eye can see only a tiny spot in high definition, and everything around it is just a blur. To take in a whole scene, your eyes must dart around wildly to scan all the important details, but you don’t notice this happening. Instead, you perceive a fully-integrated three dimensional scene with a uniformly high level of detail. I think the right way to think about this is that your brain is constantly maintaining a simulation of the world around you that is consistent with the sensory data you take in. So, yeah, for the most part, what you see is “really there.” Your brain is trying to represent reality faithfully. But you only see the image after some very heavy processing, and most of the image is actually a mix of historical data (what it looked like a moment ago), inference (like with depth perception), and expectations (filling in detail from your knowledge of the world), not an accurate representation of the light currently hitting your retinas.

It seems like you’ve considered theory of mind enough already that you immediately saw the “pilot in the cockpit” as a problematic straw person, which is what it was intended to be. You’re right that it doesn’t make sense that there’s a little you inside of you, but that is the point I was trying to make. 🙂

What other species depend on big brains for survival? Perhaps I phrased this poorly. I meant to emphasize that relatively few species depend on having a brain at all, let alone a big fancy one. In general, any organism that has a brain depends on it, though there are interesting examples of animals carrying on even after their brains are injured or destroyed.

As for the blueprint analogy, the main points I wanted to make were how much autonomy each cell has, and how the design and operation of the body only emerges from the coordinated activity of all those cells. There is no top-down master plan for the body, though you might claim that such a plan exists implicitly, distributed across the recipe books in all those cells.

I look forward to learning more about developmental biology! It’s a fascinating subject. Would you recommend the book you’re reading? I’m not sure whether to read something now or wait until grad school and take a class.

It would be tricky to construct a cell from scratch, but there’s a more subtle point I’m trying to make. DNA does not make cells. Cells make cells, and use DNA in the process. The weird consequence of this is that not everything that the cell is or does is necessarily represented in the DNA at all. Much of a cell’s behavior is actually determined by the machinery that interprets DNA, and that machinery replicates itself along with the DNA in mitosis. DNA is not all you need for life. It’s just an inert, lifeless molecule until it gets injected into a working cell. If we broadcast the human genome out into space, aliens could never make a human being from that data.

I’ll definitely say more about the mind in future posts. In the meantime, let me try to make myself more clear. Each organism is a machine that observes the world and reacts to it, cell by cell. This works great for certain kinds of bodies that are small or don’t have to move themselves in very complicated ways (microbes, fungi, plants). However, if you want a big body with articulated joints that moves quickly, then you need some sort of coordinated planning, or your cells will just trip all over themselves. That’s what the mind does. It represents the self and the environment in an integrated simulation. Your consciousness is a part of that simulation, a bit like in The Matrix. You aren’t conscious of everything that happens in your body, but your mind has a scope of awareness that encompasses the whole body. None of the cells can do that, which is why the body needs this simulation to make global plans.
