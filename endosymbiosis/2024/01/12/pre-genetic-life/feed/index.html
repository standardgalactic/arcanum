]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2024/01/12/pre-genetic-life/comment-page-1/#comment-257 Sun, 14 Jan 2024 14:48:30 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=229#comment-257 In reply to Thorin N. Tatge.

Some very interesting thoughts! Let me reply one by one.

Viruses are very weird, but some of the ideas in this blog are useful for discussing them. If a cell is an evolved machine with an evolved program (DNA), then a virus is just an evolved program. Many people don’t consider them alive because they have just one half of the equation. They don’t do anything until another life form “runs the program.” If you think of life more expansively, though, perhaps it’s more a kind of symbiosis. The virus can’t live on its own, but it can live by uniting with another life form, with a range of positive or negative consequences to the host. I’m not sure what you’re getting at regarding what the existence of RNA viruses implies. Could you say more?

The My Little Pony reference is silly, but also relevant, at least to the larger context of this blog. It makes me think of relaxed and artificial selection. That is, if nature normally selects against some trait or behavior (being pastel pink attracts predators), but some cultural invention (technology or magic) makes it safe to have that trait, suddenly it can emerge and thrive. It could even become desirable (ponies like bright colors and cutie marks!) and promoted by society (preferential treatment, selective breeding, ritualization) until it’s so strongly selected for that it dominates the population. Similar explanations have been posited for human language, for instance! It illustrates a very important point: once life becomes intelligent enough, it can use its own intelligence and preferences to reshape selective pressures and thus reshape whole species. It’s a powerful idea!

I think the word “shadow biosphere” applies here. I guess that if one existed, we might want to make finer distinctions. Like, separating alien, synthetic, and primordial forms of non-genetic life. But until we know that any of those exist, it feels weird to try to tease them apart.

How would scientists prove that there is no prebiotic / shadow-biosphere life in some sample? That’s a great question. One answer is: you can’t prove a negative. We could always find something weird that needs a new explanation, but if we don’t find it, we can never be sure that we didn’t just miss it. The other big problem with this question is: what is life? Like, scientists are getting good now at sifting through buckets of mixed DNA. If we assume all life has DNA, then they can make definitive statements about whether they can account for all the species in a sample, for instance. But what if there’s non-DNA based life? Well, then we have to look for something else… but what? There are some good guesses at molecules that any life would need, or that we think can’t occur without life making it, but these are just guesses.

Assembly Theory is an interesting attempt at quantifying just how complex a process is needed to make a given molecule. They hope to use that to identify signs of extraterrestrial life. The basic idea is that the only way we know for really complex molecules to form in any real quantity is if a living thing makes them. There are a couple of problems with this, though. First you pick a molecule, then you run some very slow and costly calculations to figure out how complex it is. So what molecules do we pick to analyze and hunt for? We’re back to a guessing game. In theory, a scientist could catalog every molecule in a sample, compute their “assembly index,” and see if there’s some unexpected complex molecules in the mix. But we just don’t have the technology to do that, yet. We can’t sort through individual molecules, and we don’t have a computer powerful enough to run the math on some of the macro-molecules we see in nature. In principle it’s possible, though, and the wider a net we cast the more likely we are to find something. Still, no way to know whether something slipped through the gaps in the net.

This whole question reminds me, in a silly way, of the question of My Little Ponies. In the world of the cartoons, there’s this species of sapient equine that come in evolutionarily impractical colors and all have these marks on their rumps indicating their personal passions and capabilities. Some of them even have magical horns and/or wings. How is one to imagine this came about? Maybe some early intelligent horse first somehow tapped into whatever made magic possible, and this led to eventual improvements in which the species made themselves colorful and clean, and eventually gave themselves cutie marks for some useful reason, and it was so adaptive that ordinary coat colors and -not- having cutie marks washed out, and now those early ponies are lost even to antiquity. Much like versions of ourselves who lack certain features now innately associated with humanity.

It certainly seems most immediately sensible to assume whatever came before the genetic system is long gone. Casualty to any number of developments, along with many other branches. But, the idea of it hanging on somewhere where it’s very strong because that’s where it -grew up- is a nifty one. I’ve heard that archea (formerly archeabaacteria) are super hard to find, and they’re a whole mysterious domain of life! So, this is a believable circumstance. If a precursor to DNA-based life is around somewhere, would that be what they call a shadow biosphere? Or does that term refer only to life that’s completely unrelated to what we know? If the latter, then it needs its own term. A shadow bioslum? An antique biosphere?

How would we be able to take a sample of living and non-living material, however small and with however much time to look through it, and rule out the idea that it has some other, more primordial form of life mixed in with it? It’s the sort of thing one assumed scientists know how to do. But maybe they don’t? If there were such a separate branch of the great living family tree, it seems like they would have to create another entire classification level to put it in, much like they created domains to go above kingdoms, because just giving it a domain or kingdom of its own doesn’t seem like it would be significant enough.
