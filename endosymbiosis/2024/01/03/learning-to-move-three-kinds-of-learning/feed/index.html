]]> By: rachelgrey749548df9a https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2024/01/03/learning-to-move-three-kinds-of-learning/comment-page-1/#comment-249 Sat, 06 Jan 2024 17:42:27 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=225#comment-249 Hey Nate, the other comment is interesting in itself — I for one (or, rather, for two) also find it fascinating how the body learns. I’ve had a long and unsteady athletic journey from skinny, deeply uncoordinated kid toward some skill in partner acrobatics and handstands, none of which really even STARTED to begin until I started lifting weights as a young adult. Many many years later I read a book called The Out-of-Sync Child, about kids who aren’t very coordinated or otherwise have communication issues between their bodies and brains; when I read that uncoordinated kids are often treated by having them carry heavy weights around, in order to amplify the signals sent by their proprioceptive systems, I had a real aha moment. Like oh wow, weightlifting really did fix me and this was the mechanism, though I had no idea what I was doing.

I have many stories of nonlinear system learning, in which some sequence of muscle firings just felt awful / inefficient / not useful until one day they magically did, and my intellectual understanding was utterly leapfrogged by those moments. There were the first 16 weeks in a row I tried to do weighted squats and they were terrible, followed by the week they felt like a dance and my weight quickly doubled. There was the time I went to PT and learned to fire my glute before my hamstring while walking — similar to your experience with walking up stairs. I could only FEEL that firing of muscles when I had a therapist literally poking me in each muscle to amplify the signal, but after about 1000 reps of conscious one-and-then-the-other, a faster motion did begin to reverse the unconscious firing order. There was, and this is classic, learning to juggle a standard 3-ball cascade, which notoriously feels overwhelming and out of control until it doesn’t.

After all these years, I find it difficult to judge to what extent a skill is going to suddenly improve via this sort of process (better signaling, proprioception, something) and to what extent it is going to just improve linearly and grindingly through the more predictable process of muscle growth (or stretching). I’d especially like to know this about press handstands, where I’m still awaiting the breakthrough after many years.

Have you heard the phrase “Neurons that fire together, wire together”? Reading up on myelin formation begins to give some notion of how practice works. I liked the book The Talent Code, although it’s pretty old at this point and some of the science may well have been eclipsed.

Yeah, sorry this post is less relatable to folks who have a different relationship to fitness. I’m aware that not everyone will feel the same way, or see the same kind of responsiveness in their body. Still, I hope talking about my experience of it may be helpful.

The main reason this made exercise more enjoyable is that I learned to see the discomfort differently. Initially, I saw it as “this sucks” or “my body wants me to stop.” Now I see it as a form of communication. My muscles are constantly telling me about their levels of readiness, strength, mobility, exhaustion, etc., how those things are changing, and how close I am to my physical limits. It’s a kind of communication which conveys a lot of nuance about the body’s state that’s hard to put into words. It sucks that these messages often feel unpleasant, but it helps to see them as messages to interpret, not just pain. Easier said than done, I know. ðŸ˜›

I struggled with dance the same way you struggled with yoga. I think I have an easier time copying someone’s movement (which is why yoga works for me) but I just can not follow a rhythm. I feel a little bad that I gave up on dance so quickly, but I had such a strong sense of “this isn’t working for me at all” that I hope I made the right choice. I figure, everyone has to find what works for them…

You don’t think it’s cool that your brain and body have multiple parallel tracks of understanding and adaptation that blend together seamlessly? Or that your mind and body do smart things for you, automatically, without you having to ask or even notice? Man, I think that’s just the coolest. It’s a marvelous machine to study, and it really makes me wonder about my conscious mind, and how it relates to me as a complex whole. Maybe you’re just already used to the idea, or don’t care as much about non-cognitive kinds of intelligence? Part of why I find this so cool is that I’m interested in learning theory and AI technology, which currently don’t account for this sort of thing at all. It’s part of our everyday human experience, common across the tree of life, yet somehow unexplained and often dismissed by folks who claim to have the answers.
