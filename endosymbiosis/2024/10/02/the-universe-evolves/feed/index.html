Oops, I can’t directly reply to your reply above, but here’s what I wrote:

I think it can’t be a matter of luck that the universe is understandable. üôÇ Some of it is intuitive and some definitely isn’t–the moon (and airplanes) in the sky, tectonic plates crunching‚Ä¶ the idea that life wasn’t designed just for us by things like us. You could say, as Hume observed, that it’s lucky that induction works at all, that patterns exist. But a world without patterns would be so utterly different that it feels wrong to say we could exist in it at all. An anthropic argument seems to almost necessitate that the universe has to have rules.

That doesn’t mean it has to have rules all the way down. It doesn’t mean that every observable phenomenon has to have a reason it happens. But if there are some weird, complex things that just ARE, and there’s no reason for them‚Ä¶ well, I’d have to wonder, why is THAT the case? I would expect there to be a reason for the lack of reasons. And if there isn’t one, I would expect a reason for the lack of reasons for the lack of reasons.

It seems that you’re saying anthropic arguments are unsatisfying only because they aren’t conclusive. Well, lots of arguments are far from conclusive, but they do offer evidence that make some idea (like life on other planets) more or less likely. I think the basic anthropic principle offers something that isn’t evidence, exactly, but is still a form of explanatory power, based on a comparison of hypotheses. One hypothesis is that living worlds are pretty common, and we just haven’t happened to find any yet, and another very different hypothesis is that they’re actually very rare–so rare that if we posit a multiverses of cosmoses, they occur in only a small fraction of those cosmoses, and usually no more than one in each. At first intuition, the second hypothesis may seem pretty unlikely compared to the first one. But the anthropic principle gives the second hypothesis a lot more credence, because it’s no longer possible to use the fact that we have a living world in front of us as direct mathematical evidence against it.

I like where you’re going with this “pseudo-existence” idea! Humans have this deep desire for the universe to make intuitive sense to us. We want to understand it, and we’ve been lucky in that, for the most part, that’s possible. It has allowed us to discover things through inference, using tools like Occam’s Razor. Or, perhaps it has nothing to do with luck. Perhaps we evolved brains that are good at recognizing and interpreting the kind of regularity that exists around us. Perhaps the universe makes sense to us because we evolved to make sense of it! But you’re right that there’s no reason the universe should make sense, and perhaps our brains aren’t evolved to make sense of, say, quantum mechanics. Perhaps there are parts of the universe that are completely beyond our comprehension. But, does that mean the universe really is arbitrary, messy, and over-complicated, or is simplicity in the eye of the beholder? Perhaps any system can be intuitive, if you have a mind that’s honed to make sense of that system. Perhaps being “intuitive” is just an alignment between the system being observed, and the system doing the observing.

Yes, you understand what I mean by “anthropic arguments.” I think they’re unsatisfying because they don’t actually answer the questions that matter most to us. Why are we here? In part, because “here” is the kind of place where we can be. Sure. That explains why we are not there, floating in open space. But it doesn’t actually explain why we are here. There could be vast numbers of Earth-like worlds in our universe. Should we expect to find Earth-like life there? Anthropic arguments can only tell us “maybe.” They can’t explain the specific reasons why we did arise here‚Äîthat is, why the possibility of life became the actuality of life. Why is the universe like we see? Because if it weren’t, we wouldn’t see it. But that doesn’t tell us what other possible universes might support life, what other kinds of life are possible, or how this universe came to be supportive of life. It’s one of those arguments that feels both clever and dumb at the same time.

Quantum physics is mind-boggling. On the Origin of Time is pretty easy to read, but very hard to grok in its entirety. That’s okay, though. I keep reading more about quantum stuff, and I feel slightly less baffled every time. Maybe I’m just getting used to the befuddlement üòâ

I’m actually reading a lovely (if rather long and technical) paper right now, by some of my favorite researchers (https://www.mdpi.com/1099-4300/26/9/765). I bring it up because it’s about “adaptability,” and they define it as “learning to solve problems better with experience.” I like this a lot. Perhaps fire can “solve” certain problems. For instance, you could imagine folding an origami shape that represents an optimization problem, such that the speed at which it burns tells you the answer to the problem. But if you did it again, you’d just get the same answer. It would never spontaneously find a better answer, or find a better way to compute the answer with repetition. That would be “non-trivial adaptation,” which is a thing life does, but not most physical systems.

Saying “life is a super-charged way of generating entropy” isn’t very satisfying, in part because lots of processes do this, life’s just “better” at it in some unspecified way. I think people suggest this because it assigns a sort of “purpose” to life that arises out of pure physics. But I like this discussion of “adaptability” better, because it highlights the difference between trivial optimization and learning (which fire or crystals or whatever can do) and the non-trivial kind (which only very particular physical systems with complex inner structure can do). Viruses do this. They get better at hijacking cells with experience. It’s still unclear whether they’re “alive,” though. Perhaps that is the wrong question to ask?
