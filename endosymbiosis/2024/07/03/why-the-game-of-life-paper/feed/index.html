You’re right that it’s pretty clear the genome doesn’t literally describe a body, but in the early days of evolutionary algorithms, that’s how we did it. What we’ve found is it doesn’t work very well. Making some tiny change to a literal description of a body usually breaks things. However, if we use what’s called a “generative encoding,” where the genes describe how to make a body, then maybe that small change controls something meaningful (like the length of a leg) and is more likely to be neutral or beneficial, so evolution will progress faster. This is the insight I’m talking about. You’re right, it’s kinda obvious biologically, but it took a long time to figure out how to do it in a computer. Now I’m saying, “Yes, a generative encoding! But we shouldn’t define that encoding. We should find a way to evolve that part, too!”

How does a leg know to stop growing? Believe it or not, this is still an open question to science. But I know some partial answers. The classic one, which is talked about a bit in Endless Forms Most Beautiful, is timed gene expression and chemical sensing. Cells coordinate by using chemical signals, either generated by their neighbors (hey, we’re all “handing” right now, join in!) or by the endocrine system (hey, it’s puberty time, time for a growth spurt!) These trigger patterns of growth and stopping that are very precisely timed. They run for a certain amount of time. Different cells grow at particular rates and delays relative to one another. This plays a big role. A newer discovery is the impact of bioelectric fields. It seems that the body produces a global pattern of electricity that in some way maps out the shape and limits of the body. Cells sense this, and use it to guide their growth. But, seriously, how bodies grow is spectacularly complicated and we definitely haven’t figured it all out.

Yes, large language models are a kind of deep learning. üôÇ There are too many names for this stuff!

I think there probably is some of that “sub-contracting” that you describe, but I think of “gut feelings” a little differently. Kind of the opposite, actually. My perspective is that life has had preferences, made decisions, and felt a drive to act since it was a single cell. That sort of intelligence has been further developed and complexified by the evolution of bodies and then, eventually, very late in the game, brains. So, I don’t think the brain is primarily responsible for those things, though its cognitive insights certainly play a big role in influencing them. I see the brain as a tool for the body. The body knows what its physical needs are, and feels an urge to act. It defers to the brain to interpret a situation (and thus how to feel about it), decide what action to take (by imagining how it would feel in various scenarios), and how to implement that action (how the cells of the body should prepare and behave). That said, this is also relatively uncharted territory for science. What’s clear is that the mind and body are both involved, and interact in complex ways.

Yes, you’re right, evolution is about both problem solving and problem finding. I emphasized problem finding, because it’s often neglected, and because I believe it’s more important in the long run. What use is problem solving if you haven’t found the right problem? How can a species survive without constantly adapting as the problem landscape changes? But, ultimately, the purpose of finding a problem is to solve it and reap the benefits.

Your to-do list is a great example! I think life tries to strike this balance at many levels. Building that into your plans is good for you and your brain!

Regarding the algorithm: that’s what I’m working on! To be perfectly honest, I’m still trying to understand what I built better by dissecting it, rebuilding it, and describing it in fine detail. If all goes well, I’ll have much more to say, once I’ve identified what “really matters” to this program.

]]> By: Nate Gaylinn (he/him) https://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/2024/07/03/why-the-game-of-life-paper/comment-page-1/#comment-275 Mon, 15 Jul 2024 11:02:09 +0000 http://thinkingwithnate.wordpress.com/?p=240#comment-275 In reply to Thorin N. Tatge.

I like your suggestion that this is second- or third-order evolution that I’m studying. I’m not sure, though, because I’m still trying to put a finer point on how my approach differs from others! Most of the work on “evolvability” so far seems related to the question: “what if the genome doesn’t literally describe the body, but instead describes how to make a body?” That’s pretty cool and important, but I’m asking a different question: “what if the genome doesn’t mean anything at all, but form and behavior emerges from the interaction of co-evolving cell and genes?”

I must admit, I do have a bias towards anthropomorphism. Or, more precisely, the intentional stance. I think evolution is how life became purposeful and self-directed, so I think it’s actually valid and useful to apply those terms in the context of early evolution, even if what early life did was the simplest possible version of that, and under the hood it’s all just deterministic physics.

Deep learning is pretty cool, but I think it’s important to remember: it couldn’t possibly exist on its own. What brains and deep learning systems do is model, predict, and guide the systems they observe. They don’t really do anything on their own. For instance, I strongly suspect that what makes people want to do things or decide whether something is good or bad is literally “gut feelings,” coming from the body. The brain is important for triggering those bodily sensations in appropriate contexts, and interpreting them when they show up, but I don’t think it could decide on anything without the body for guidance. It also depends on the body for perceiving and interacting with the world. There’s so much “infrastructure” that’s needed for a brain to be useful. That had to evolve first, and in some ways its much more complicated and intelligent than a brain.

In the study of adaptive systems, there’s often talk of a tradeoff between “exploration” and “exploitation.” Basically, if you find an opportunity, you could stop looking and spend all your energy exploiting that opportunity, or you could keep exploring to try and find an even better opportunity. Usually you want a balance between these strategies, but the right balance depends on context. Also, different algorithms do better at different parts of this. Deep learning is great at exploitation, because it uses gradient descent to hone right in on the opportunities it finds. It’s less good at finding all the possible alternatives. Traditional evolutionary algorithms are great at exploration, but not that “honing in” part. My hope is to make an evolutionary algorithm that’s good at both, and at finding a suitable balance between them.

I think you have a good idea of what sort of test cases I want to build! Combinations of diverse tasks. Perhaps even combinations that shift over time or space! This may be challenging for me to build and for my algorithm to navigate. But I have a hunch that my algorithm should be able to do this, and if so, I need a way to test it.

You know, now that you mention it, my algorithm is actually a mix of expanding / restricting the search space. I give my simulated life an unconstrained search space, but then the first thing it does is narrow that space down to some random, tiny segment of what’s possible. Then, over time, it gradually adds more dimensions to the search space. This opens up new possibilities, but also creates new ways to constrain. For instance, if it adds a new dimension that says, “stretch out whatever pattern evolves” then the constraint would be “how far should I stretch / squash the pattern?” Or if it’s “repeat the pattern in a line,” the constraint might be “what line and how many repetitions?” I think this has a very interesting resemblance to natural evolution.
