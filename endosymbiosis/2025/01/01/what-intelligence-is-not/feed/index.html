I think I ought to write more about the intelligence of cells and microbes specifically, because it really is quite impressive. I think what makes this sort of intelligence hard to recognize is that it doesn’t fit our usual framing. Like, nitrogen fixing bacteria doesn’t choose to fix nitrogen, they don’t “know” how to do it, and they’re not “figuring out” anything new when they do, it’s just some rote behavior. And yet, from an evolutionary perspective, this microbe solved a central problem to living here on Earth.

Nitrogen is an extremely valuable nutrient, and there’s plenty of it, just in a form that’s useless without some clever chemistry. Only a handful of species have figured out the trick to extract nitrogen from the air, and they are near the root of basically every food chain on the planet. We all depend on that work. And it is creative work, too. These microbes had to discover nitrogen fixing, realize its value, and optimize it for efficient production. They had to invent tools for manipulating, managing, and integrating nitrogen into their own biological processes. To find their niche, they had to co-evolve with plants, adapting to their needs, striking a metabolic compromise, negotiating “treaties” for trading rare nutrients and bargaining for access to protected spaces. I’m being rather anthropomorphic here, but that’s just because we don’t really have a good vocabulary for discussing this kind of adaptation and innovation. I agree with you that we could use more and better words! But it’s a mistake to think this stuff “just happens” or isn’t as “intelligent” as what humans do. The main difference is microbes learn, “think,” and behave collectively, on different time scales, and using different mechanisms.

If a species could get more intelligent without having to pay for it, wouldn’t that be better? I don’t know. It’s kind of a strange question, because you always have to pay. We evolve intelligence to get around the constraints of the world, so if we had fewer metabolic constraints, there would be less need for intelligence, not more. I do see your point, though. My first thought is to look to trees. Would they be better off if they had greater personal intelligence? I mean, maybe if they could act in the moment to shoo a predator, or get better access to the sun, they would be better off. But they do that already, just slowly! To go faster, they’d need muscles and eyes and brains or something like that, but would that be worth it? I guess if we’re giving it all away for free, then why not, but the tree’s current level of intelligence seems very effective for its life strategy.

You make good points about the cost of a big brain, but it’s also worth considering its value. We take it for granted that brains are good, but what do they really do? They help us build and maintain real-time internal models of the external world. Seemingly, the main purpose of that is to let us run thought experiments instead of acting in real life, which is safer and uses fewer resources. But you also need to take the time and effort to develop that mental model. You need to stop and consider your actions before you take them. If your mental model doesn’t align with the real world, that can be a big problem. There are plenty of lifestyles where this isn’t of much benefit, or could even be counterproductive. And if you’re willing to be small or slow, you can do all those things in different ways, without neurons.

Yeah, that’s a very interesting question! What is the cognitive value of language outside of cultural transmission? I don’t think we have any idea. Part of the answer probably lies in the evolutionary precursors or prerequisites for the language faculty, but we don’t understand that very well either.

Yes, I would argue that we need nature to keep innovating, exploring, and producing not just the things we need, but robust ecosystems surrounding the things we need, and whatever nature needs for its own wellbeing.

As for “control” and “rights” I think they are just human fictions. We do not have “control” over nature, much as we like to pretend we do. We have power over nature. We can force it to do some things with violence, but not all things, and it may push back in various ways, including by dying away instead of complying. Do we have the “right” to do that? We have the power to. Nobody’s gonna stop us, except ourselves. As you say, it’s a matter of what we think is moral, and how well we hold ourselves to those moral standards. However, I think we have done (and still do!) a lot to minimize and avoid this moral quandary, looking away from it so we can continue to exploit nature without guilt or compromise.

Ah yes, punctuated equilibrium I’ve heard of. I recall it was an idea supported by Stephen Jay Gould. And it makes sense, as human history also has punctuated equilibrium or something close to it, and it’s fun to think of those periods of brief upheaval. Still, the question remains… did (maximum) intelligence evolve evenly in time with these punctuated periods of rapid evolution? Was it AS steady as the rest of evolution?

“I think when intelligence is lost, it’s more often from suddenly not having enough calories and going extinct, rather than scaling back brains to save calories, but I’m no expert on that.” Yes, my intuition agrees! That’s almost macabre.

Interesting to learn about IQ being even more limited in its ability to put things in perspective than I realized.

Your remarks about nitrogen-fixing bacteria incited me to impulsively draft a smol observer poem. Yet it still puzzles me that you consider this biological capability a form of intelligence. Would you call it intelligence whenever any process in any creature’s body changes some chemical to some other chemical? When the body works the way it’s evolved to work, e.g. when platelets seal up a cut? I guess the immune system targeting invaders is a type of intelligence, and I can see how that makes sense. But just the fact that a creature can eat what it eats… if that’s intelligence, it seems to me we really need a really to distinguish traditional mental intelligence from the other kinds.

When I said “I think more intelligence is better,” I didn’t mean for a species… I meant for myself, really, or for some hypothetical other being. More intelligence will tend to make individuals happier, since… with some exceptions, when we get more ways to manipulate information, we can take them (if they help) or leave them (if they don’t). But now I need to go back and remind myself what we were talking about. Ah, you said that as a society we tend to think that more intelligence is better, and I guess you meant for whole species or larger groups of living things. Well, I do think if a species could get more intelligent without having to lose brain space or burn more calories for it, that would probably help that species do better, whatever kind of thing it might be. Do you disagree?

“Part of what makes big brains risky is you get to have so many fewer of them that extinction is more likely.” That’s a good observation. And it’s the fact that big brains use up resources that limits them, right? I guess here it’s not just the energy necessary to build and maintain the brains, but also the space they take up in an egg or womb. So if intelligence is inherently better, it’s with “all other things being equal,” which of course they aren’t.

I take your point that our capacity for culture and language came from relatively small genetic changes and that our brains aren’t all -that- different from the brains of other vertebrates, but the consequences of those differences were unusually vast. “Does language make us much more intelligent? No,” you write. By saying ‘much’, you indicate that in some way, language itself does make us more intelligent, if only a little. I find that interesting, in a Sapir-Whorf kind of way. I’d think that if something like metabolizing alcohol counts as intelligence, the sort of transformation language has on our thoughts does make us much more intelligent, since it lets us do so many new things and also lets us think in so many new ways. I mean, it’s hard to know for sure, since I can’t erase language from my mind and try thinking about the same things I’m used to thinking about. But, if I can solve a tricky puzzle that a gorilla can’t solve, I’d bet language would have a lot to do with it. Even if I haven’t learned the answer from someone else, I may have talked it out in my own head.

I agree with your warning about LLMs, though, and how they’re -all- language and therefore not really smarter than animals.

So, preserving parts of nature is in our selfish best interest. But it also makes sense to do more than preserve: to allow nature’s designs a certain precedence over the world, and to let it keep designing sustainable systems. Does that mean we don’t have the right to control nature? I guess that’s a valid way to put it. I tend to balk at statements about who has the right to do things or doesn’t have the right to do things, because it seems to me that until society agrees, we’re just arguing about whether someone should have the right, and not whether they actually do; and also because having (or not having) a particular right can be taken in many different ways.

“[W]e tend to think that more intelligence is better, but that’s just not the case. This reminds me of one time I was a child, riding in a car with some other children, and one of my sister’s friends noticed a cow out the window and said something like “I wish I were a cow. They seem happy and they don’t have to think.” This led to some discussion about happiness and whether intelligence is better. Well, most people (including Ogden Nash) would not prefer to be a cow, regardless of whether they might be free-range or content or safe from butchery. Why is that? Well, what would it mean for ME to be a cow? Aside from bodily changes, it would mean taking my more complex human mental life and stripping most of it away. That seems really sad and a lot like death. In short, intelligence is better for us intelligent things, and lack of intelligence is probably better for less intelligence things, because it’s better, in a preferential sense, for most creatures to remain about how they are. To change too drastically would be, in a way, not to exist anymore at all.

But if you can adjust your level of intelligence without dramatically changing the sort of being you are, I think more intelligence is better. Just like more strength and more health and more agility are better.

Human intelligence has caused a lot of problems for the planet, true. But those problems tend to be the result of runaway success. We spread out so much that we started to fight for land and wage wars. We dominated other living things so much that we wiped out species and ecosystems and are damaging the world’s climate. How to deal with tremendous and sudden biological success is an unsolved problem. But… if some other species somehow outcompeted and dominated the planet’s other living things to a similar extent WITHOUT intelligence… wouldn’t they have caused most of the same problems, but without the wherewithal to hold back and start doing things like protecting parts of the natural world?

What universal intelligence metric could dolphins beat us on that we haven’t essentially tried? There are specific tests they could beat us on that might be called kinds of intelligence… maybe recognizing tonal pitch? Maybe some form of social intelligence? But where would one find the argument that these niche-seeming metrics are where ‘real’ intelligence lies?

It’s an interesting thing to say that our society, rather than our intelligence, sets us apart from other species. Comparing an unsocialized, wild human barely managing to scrape by to a dolphin or octopus or some social primate does seem to make a stark point. Yet… lots of species, really every species, have environmental conditions they depend on in order to thrive. Being social is one of ours. Isn’t it fair to say that our propensity for passing knowledge to each other, our command of language and culture, are biological features that make us more intelligent? At the least, it would seem that they’re biological features that give us the potential to be much more intelligent.

Yes, some other species can co-exist with us without language. But the plants and animals that occupy our cities are the lucky ones, which I’ve heard called ‘clean-up species’, that outcompete everything else in a human-occupied setting and reduce overall biodiversity. (Yes, not everyone lives in a city-like environment.) The creatures that are good at existing with us are the ones we have a much easier time co-existing harmoniously with!

“Or, we can perpetuate the myth that we are special to justify excluding and exploiting nature instead.” Some people do this, certainly–it seems like a dated narrative to me, but it’s a familiar one. The thing is… do we NEED to justify excluding and exploiting nature? Having to justify something implies there’s been an objection raised that needs to be overcome–so, what objection are we assuming is present here, that the justification of being superintelligent is overcoming?

“Similarly, if anything less than human is just a dumb resource for us to exploit, why not pave the planet? What’s wrong with processing all of that biomass, every living thing on Earth, into fuel and plastics?” For one thing, we need other living things to live. More to the point, though, we also benefit spiritually, scientifically, healthwise and culturally from having thriving ecologies exist. In the absence of thriving ones, semi-thriving, semi-constructed ecologies are better than collapsed ones. You can say it’s because life has the right to exist and we’re not special, or you can say we should preserve a good chunk of nature because that’s the BEST way to exploit it. It’s more varied, more sustainable, and so on.
